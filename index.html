Correlations Between Age, Income, Poverty Level Rate & Health Risks

Poverty and Obesity

There seems to be little correlation between household income in the U.S. and obesity. The data does suggest that America is struggling with obesity in general.

Poverty and Smoking

There does appear to be an inflection point showing increased risks of smoking in states where 18% or more of the population find themselves impoverished.

Poverty and the Uninsured

It would appear that there is not a strong correlation between income and those who are insured. Texas has the highest number of uninsured adults, but does not have the highest number of families in poverty. Therefore, there must be other factors that contribute to whether an individual is insured, or not.

Age and Obesity

There does appear to be slight correlation between a states median age and level of obesity. It seems that higher age groups are more susceptible to being obese, but that is likely not always the case. Older age groups are typically less active than younger groups, and would therefore do much to explain why an older person is more likely to be obese. Clearly there are other factors, like poverty and income that may affect obesity levels.

Age and Smoking

It does appear that states with a large proportion of older age groups tend to have the highest rates of smoking. Perhaps states where the median age is higher have individuals that have smoked for many years, and have chosen not to quit. As opposed to states like Utah where the median age is very low, and much of the population doesn't smoke at all.

Age and the Uninsured

Compared with other age groups, young adults are the most likely to go without coverage. This can be expected since young adults tend to be much healthier than other age groups, and can typically risk being uninsured. Massachusetts appears to have the lowest number of uninsured individuals, while Texas has the highest rate, but a much lower median age. An interesting case is Florida, where the median age is much higher than other states at 42, but has much higher rates of poverty

Income and Obesity

Overall obesity prevalence does tend to decrease with increased levels of income. Just as poverty affects the availability of healthy food options, as does income. Lower income residents likely do not have the same access to low cost healthy food choices as does higher income groups.

Income and Smoking

A household's income contributes directly to the level of poverty. Therefore, just as states with high rates of poverty tend to have a greater population of individuals that smoke, states with lower median household income will tend to also have higher rates of smoking. States with relatively higher median incomes have some of the lowest rates of smoking

Income and the Uninsured

Many uninsured people cite the high cost of insurance as the main reason they lack coverage. Most uninsured people are in low-income families and have at least one worker in the family. Reflecting the more limited availability of public coverage in some states. This is quite clearly the case in Texas where the level of uninsured individuals is the highest in the country.
